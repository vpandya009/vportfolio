Case Study
01 Understanding the Space

A platform with no product inside it

Realize is an enterprise cybersecurity platform built around four core capabilities: running security assessments, deploying solutions, managing incidents, and automating tasks through AI agents. The technical foundation was solid. But when I arrived, the product didn't exist.

What I found on day one was essentially a landing page with four unlabelled entry points. The screens had been AI-generated and handed directly to engineering — no UX logic, no user flows, no structure. Nobody had designed the system. Nobody had even asked what order the modules should be used in.

The starting point

A security team logging into Realize for the first time saw four unnamed module tiles and no signal of where to begin. Most couldn't navigate past the first screen without asking for help. The platform had features. It had no product experience.

02 My Role

What my work actually covered

I came in as the sole designer on this project. There was no design team, no existing design system, no prior UX work to build from. I owned the entire design process — from initial research through to final UI delivery.

🔬 Research

AI-assisted secondary research, competitive analysis of enterprise security tools, and direct user interviews with security professionals to understand real workflows.

🗂️ Synthesis

Affinity mapping, persona creation, problem statement definition, HMW framing, and translating raw insights into design opportunities.

🏗️ Architecture

Designed the full information architecture — how four modules connect, how users move between them, and how AI acts as the layer reducing complexity throughout.

🎨 Design Execution

Wireframes, visual language, a full design system, and high-fidelity screens for all key flows including onboarding, dashboard, deploy, manage, agents, and the AI chatbot.

03 Initial Problem Framing

The brief I was handed

The problem statement I received at the start was broad but real: users cannot understand the platform. They log in, they see modules, and they don't know what to do. They don't know where to start, what order to follow, or what any given action will result in.

The brief as given
"Users can't understand the platform — they don't know what to do when they log in."
This was true, but it described a symptom. The deeper question was: why can't they understand it, and what would it take to fix that at a system level — not just a surface level?
↓
What I suspected after the founder call
"The platform was built around capabilities, not around how people actually think about security work."
Security professionals think in workflows: I have a problem → I need to understand it → I need to fix it. The four modules mapped to steps in that workflow, but the product didn't make that logic visible. I needed research to validate this hunch before acting on it.

With that framing in place, I went into research with a clear goal: understand how security teams actually work, what tools they use today, where they struggle, and what a platform that genuinely helps them would need to do differently.

03.5 Our Approach

Two designers, one week, a modified design sprint

Engineering was already moving. There was no time for a long research phase, no room for weeks of ideation. I was working alongside one other designer — two of us total — and we needed to go from a vague problem to a concrete platform structure as fast as responsibly possible.

We adapted the classic 5-day design sprint format to fit our constraints. Not a textbook sprint — we stretched it to a week and shaped each phase around what this project actually needed. The goal wasn't to design every screen in five days. It was to establish the right structure, logic, and design direction before a single pixel was pushed.

01
🧭 Align — founder, stakeholders, team Day 1

Before sketching anything, we needed everyone pointing at the same problem. We ran alignment sessions with the founder and the engineering team to separate vision from assumptions and make sure we were solving the right thing.

Broke down the platform vision into clear, testable goals — what success looks like for each module
Identified what the product must solve immediately vs. what can wait for v2
Surfaced conflicting assumptions between founder vision and what engineering had already built — and resolved them before designing anything
Mapped the current state: what's real, what's aspirational, and what gaps are blocking users on day one
02
🔍 Explore & benchmark Day 2

We didn't want to design in a vacuum. We studied how the best (and worst) enterprise security tools handle the same problems — not to copy them, but to understand what patterns users already know and where every competitor consistently fails.

Studied Sentinel, Cortex XSIAM, and CrowdStrike Falcon — tore apart their IA, navigation patterns, and AI integration models
Analysed how security workflows are structured in practice: assess → triage → deploy → monitor, and where that chain breaks
Identified the consistent gap across all three: strong on detection, weak on guided action — exactly where Realize could differentiate
03
👤 Define personas & workflows Day 3

We mapped the two users who matter most — the ops lead making decisions under pressure, and the engineer executing those decisions in the platform. Then we defined what each one actually needs to do, in what order, and how often.

Defined Aarav (security ops lead) and Neha (cloud security engineer) — their goals, their friction points, and their mental models
Mapped the core actions each user needs to perform and the sequence that makes sense to them — not the sequence the platform was currently offering
Prioritised flows by impact and frequency — what do users do most, and what failing to get right would cost them the most time
04
✏️ Sketch & diverge Day 4

With a shared understanding of users and constraints, we diverged — each exploring different structural approaches before comparing. The goal wasn't polish. It was breadth: find as many ways to solve the navigation and workflow problem as possible before committing to any.

Explored different ways to organise four modules into a coherent platform — linear flows, hub-and-spoke, contextual entry, AI-led navigation
Tested different approaches to the assess → deploy handoff — this was the most critical moment to get right
Focused specifically on reducing decision fatigue at entry — how do you walk into a platform and know where to start in under 5 seconds?
05
✅ Converge & commit Day 5

We brought our sketches together, stress-tested the best ideas against scalability and engineering feasibility, and committed to a direction. By end of day 5 we had the core platform structure — the logic that all subsequent design work would build on.

Evaluated ideas against three criteria: does it scale to new modules? Can engineering build it in the current stack? Does it serve both personas without compromising either?
Aligned with engineering on what could ship fast vs. what would need iteration — design direction had to be realistic, not just elegant
Finalised the four-path landing structure, the AI chatbot as connective layer, and the assess → deploy handoff as the platform's core UX logic
What we had after one week

A validated platform structure — four modules with a clear entry logic, a defined assess-to-deploy handoff, and an AI chatbot layer that connected it all. Not screens. Not pixels. A system that everyone on the team understood and agreed on. That's what made everything that came after possible.

04 Research

Finding the gaps before designing the fix

Research in this project had a real constraint: time. A full research sprint wasn't feasible — engineering was already moving. I needed to be strategic about what I could learn quickly, and rigorous about how I used it.

I ran three parallel streams: AI-assisted secondary research to quickly map the landscape, competitive analysis to understand what existing tools do well and where they fall short, and direct user interviews with security professionals to ground everything in real behaviour.

04a AI-Assisted Research

Moving fast without cutting corners

With limited time for traditional secondary research, I used Claude and Perplexity to rapidly synthesise existing knowledge — academic papers, industry reports, practitioner forums, and CISO-level content — about how enterprise security teams work and where they struggle most.

Why AI-assisted research?

When you don't have weeks for literature review, AI tools can surface patterns across hundreds of sources in hours. The goal isn't to replace rigorous research — it's to build a well-informed foundation quickly, so that the time you do spend with real users is focused on the right questions, not starting from zero.

📚 What I fed in

Security team workflow documentation, CISO pain point surveys, industry reports on SOC analyst burnout, tool fragmentation studies, and AI in security adoption trends.

🎯 What I asked for

Common workflow breakdowns, where teams lose time, what decisions are hardest to make under pressure, and where AI assistance was most and least trusted.

📍 What it surfaced

A consistent pattern: security professionals are overwhelmed not by lack of data, but by inability to act on it quickly. Too much information, too little guidance.

⚠️ How I used it

As a hypothesis-builder, not a truth-teller. Every pattern flagged by AI research was carried into user interviews as a question to validate — not an assumption to design around.

05 Synthesis

Making sense of everything I found

By the end of the research phase I had AI-generated literature patterns, competitive teardown notes, interview transcripts, and my own observations from the founder conversation — all sitting across different documents and tabs. The next step was to stop collecting and start connecting.

Synthesis is where the real design work begins. Raw findings don't tell you what to build. Synthesis shows you the patterns inside those findings — the recurring pain, the structural gaps, the moments where every source points to the same underlying problem.

05a Affinity Mapping

From scattered inputs to visible patterns

By this point I had a lot of raw material — but it was fragmented across different sources and people. I'd spoken to the founder about his vision, had conversations with engineers already building the platform, talked to security professionals about their real daily workflows, and pulled in competitive observations and AI-research patterns. Everything was in different tabs, voice notes, and documents.

Affinity mapping was how I made sense of all of it. I broke everything down into individual observations — one idea, one quote, one friction point per note — and then started grouping them by what they had in common. No pre-set categories. I let the clusters form naturally and named them only once the pattern was clear.

06a Final Problem Statement

The real problem was never the features

The original brief — "users can't understand the platform" — was pointing at a symptom, not a cause. Research forced a reframe.

What the brief assumed
"The platform needs better UI so users can understand what each module does."
This treats the problem as a presentation issue — that better labels and cleaner screens would fix it. Research showed this wasn't the core issue.
↓
What research revealed
"Security professionals don't struggle with understanding tools — they struggle with knowing what to do next and being able to do it without switching tools."
The problem isn't comprehension. It's action. Users understand what assessment means — they don't have a guided path from assessment to remediation without leaving the system or reading docs.
Validated problem statement

Enterprise security teams are not short on information or tools — they're short on guided action. Existing platforms excel at detecting and displaying problems, but leave users to manually piece together what to do next, how to do it, and in which tool. Realize had the same gap. The solution required redesigning not just the interface, but the underlying logic of how the system talks to its users.

06b How Might We

Turning problems into design questions

HMW questions are the bridge between a validated problem and an actual design direction. They're deliberately open — specific enough to point somewhere, broad enough to invite creative thinking.

How might we guide users toward the next best action instead of leaving them to explore a platform they don't understand?
How might we reduce tool-switching and bring the full security workflow — assess, deploy, manage — into one coherent system?
How might we simplify complex security deployment processes into clear, step-by-step flows that don't require documentation?
How might we use AI as a structural guide — not a bolt-on feature — that reduces cognitive load at every step?
How might we design for security leads and engineers simultaneously, when their needs and mental models are so different?

06c Solution Direction

Where design effort should go

Not every problem is equally solvable through design. These four directions had the strongest signal from research — the most frequent pain, the most users affected, and the clearest path to meaningful improvement. They became the north star for every decision that followed.

01
Guided entry & clear structure

Replace the four unlabelled tiles with a platform that tells users where they are and what to do — in under five seconds. Structure has to precede any specific feature work.

02
Connected workflow logic

Design the platform so that completing one module naturally leads into the next — assessment findings should feed directly into deployment options, not require re-navigation.

03
AI as a structural layer

Make the AI chatbot the connective tissue of the platform — the thing that bridges modules, answers questions mid-workflow, and executes actions on behalf of users.

04
Reduced deployment friction

Turn multi-day manual configuration into a guided, step-by-step flow that anyone on the team can complete — not just the most senior engineer who's done it before.

→ Four directions. Now the question was: what does the actual solution look like? That's where Ideation begins.

09 Experience Structure

Architecture before interface

Before touching a single screen in Figma, I mapped the full experience structure — how the platform is organised, how users move through it, and how each module connects to the next. Structure has to be right before visual design begins. Otherwise you're just decorating a broken system.

This is the phase most people rush through — they see wireframes as a stepping stone to the "real" design. But for a platform with four distinct modules, two very different user types, and an AI layer threaded across all of it, getting the structure wrong would have cost weeks of rework. So I slowed down here deliberately.

Why structure comes first

A beautiful screen built on a broken structure is still broken. Users don't experience pixels — they experience sequences. When those sequences don't make sense, no amount of visual polish will save the product. The structure work here directly determined how every screen was laid out, what actions appeared where, and how the modules communicated with each other.

🗺️ What I mapped

The full IA — every section, every sub-section, every entry point. How a user navigates into each module and what they encounter at each step of the journey.

🔗 How modules connect

The critical handoffs — especially Assess → Deploy. Where does one module end and another begin? What data travels between them? How does the AI chatbot bridge the gap?

👤 Two users, one system

Every structural decision was filtered through both personas. Aarav needs fast access to high-level views. Neha needs depth and step-by-step control. The structure had to serve both without compromise.

⚡ First-time vs. returning

A first-time user needs a guided path. A returning user needs direct access. The structure accounts for both — onboarding logic that fades out as users build familiarity with the system.

Core Flow 2 — Assess to Deploy (the key handoff)

This is the most important flow in the product — the moment where an assessment finding becomes a deployment action. In every competitor platform, this handoff is manual. In Realize, it's designed to be seamless.

09b Information Architecture

One platform, four clear entry points

Before the UI, I defined the full IA — what content exists, how it's grouped, and how each module's navigation relates to a shared platform structure. The goal wasn't four separate tools that happened to live in the same URL. It was one system with a clear logic for how everything connects.

The key architectural insight

The platform's four modules aren't parallel options — they're sequential stages of a security workflow. The IA had to make that sequence legible without forcing users into a rigid linear path. Power users need to jump directly. New users need the sequence to be obvious.

🔁 What stayed consistent across modules

Top navigation with module tabs, an AI chatbot accessible from anywhere, a notifications panel, and a profile/wallet section in the same position on every screen.

🎯 What changed per module

The main content area, the primary actions available, and the context-relevant AI prompts. Each module feels purpose-built for its specific task.

⚖️ The principle behind it

Every user should feel like the platform was built for what they're doing right now — not like they're using a Swiss Army knife where every tool looks the same.

🧠 What drove the hierarchy

Frequency and stakes. Assessment and Dashboard are the primary entry points — most accessed, most consequential. Deploy and Manage are secondary. Agent Factory is for power users.

💡 Outcome: A clear, module-aware navigation system where every user can immediately locate what they need — and a shared platform structure that makes the four modules feel like one coherent product.
10 Design Execution

From structure to screens

With the flows mapped and the architecture defined, it was time to build the product. Design execution moved through four distinct phases — each tightening fidelity and sharpening decisions until the final UI was ready.

This is the phase people tend to jump to first. Sketches, Figma frames, component libraries, colour palettes. But doing it after structure means every visual decision is grounded in actual user intent — not aesthetic preference. Each screen I opened in Figma already had a clear job, a defined context, and a known user. The visual work was translating that clarity into something tangible.

The feel before the interface

Before building frames, I defined the visual tone. For an enterprise security platform used by high-pressure security teams, the design couldn't feel consumer-light. But it also couldn't feel like a legacy enterprise tool — intimidating, data-dense, and dated. It needed to feel precise, capable, and just intelligent enough to trust.

Why Manrope?

Manrope is a geometric sans-serif — designed from mathematical proportions, which gives it an inherent sense of structure and logic. That felt right for a security platform where users are parsing complex data and making high-stakes decisions. The letterforms are open and clear, which means text stays legible even at the small sizes common in dense enterprise interfaces.

The design logic
  • Legibility under stress. Security teams scan fast. Manrope's generous x-height and open apertures make it readable at 11–12px — the kind of sizes appearing in data tables and status tags.
  • Weight range. With 7 weights from Light to ExtraBold, Manrope creates a clear typographic hierarchy — users can scan a screen and immediately understand what's a heading, a label, and a data point.
  • Modern but not trendy. Unlike display fonts, Manrope doesn't compete with the content. It's confident enough to feel premium, neutral enough to stay out of the way.

11 Challenges

Where real constraints broke the ideal plan

Every project has the version you design in theory and the version you have to actually ship. These were the moments where the gap between those two versions required the most careful thinking.

The core tension

Designing for security professionals means designing for extremely high stakes, high complexity, and low tolerance for ambiguity — while also being asked to simplify and guide. The challenge was making the platform feel capable and trustworthy to an expert, while still being navigable for someone encountering it for the first time.

01
Engineering constraint
Screens had already shipped before design started

Several screens had been built by the time I joined, based on AI-generated designs. I had to work within what existed while simultaneously redesigning it — which meant making engineering-impact cases clearly and quickly.

How I handled it: Prioritised changes by user impact, flagged the highest-friction ones first, and framed redesign asks as workflow fixes rather than aesthetic preferences.
02
Persona tension
Designing for two users with completely different mental models

Aarav (ops lead) needs high-level visibility and fast decision support. Neha (cloud engineer) needs detailed control and step-by-step execution. Every design decision had to serve both without compromising either.

How I handled it: Progressive disclosure throughout — surface-level summaries for Aarav with a clear path to detail, and depth-first layouts for Neha's module-specific flows. Same screens, different entry behaviours.
🖼️
Image — Challenges 01 & 02
img/Realize/challenges_01_02.png
03
AI trust design
Making AI feel trustworthy to trained sceptics

Security professionals are professionally trained to distrust systems they can't inspect. An AI that claims to "execute your deployment" sounds alarming without clear explanation of what it's actually doing at each step.

How I handled it: Every AI action was designed with three layers — a plain-language explanation of what it will do, a user confirmation step, and a live progress trace. Control was never hidden.
04
Research constraint
No access to a full enterprise research panel

I couldn't run a broad research panel with enterprise security professionals — I had two users and time pressure. AI-assisted research had to do more work than usual.

How I handled it: Treated every AI-surfaced pattern as a hypothesis, not a finding. Carried each one into user interviews as a question to validate or refute — never designed directly from AI research alone.
💡 Takeaway: The most important design decisions in this project didn't come from clear research. They came from the moments where constraints forced creative trade-offs — and required clear rationale rather than best practice.

13 Reflection

Beyond screens — what this project taught me

This project started as a brief to "fix the UI." It became something much larger — a full, from-scratch product design exercise in a domain I had to learn while building. What looked like a UI problem revealed itself as a system problem: layered, fragmented, and deeply dependent on how different types of users interact with high-stakes information under pressure.

01

Structure is the design work — not a precursor to it

The hardest and most impactful decisions in this project happened before Figma opened. Once the IA and module logic were right, every screen fell into place naturally. Jumping to UI first would have created a beautiful product with a broken logic underneath.

02

AI research is only as rigorous as the designer using it

Using Claude and Perplexity to map the landscape quickly was genuinely effective — but only because every output was treated as a hypothesis, not a fact. The discipline of separating "AI found this pattern" from "a user confirmed this problem" kept the research credible even under time pressure.

03

Designing AI features means designing for trust, not just usability

Security professionals are trained sceptics. "The AI will deploy this for you" is not reassuring — it's alarming. The real design problem was making every AI action transparent, interruptible, and auditable. Trust is a design layer, not an afterthought.

04

Being the only designer forces clarity you don't get in a team

No design reviews means you have to internalise every critique yourself. Every decision I made had to be fully reasoned before I moved on — because there was no one else to catch it. That constraint made the rationale tighter, and the decisions faster.

🖼️

14 Future Iterations

This Is Just the Start

This version of Realize is an MVP — it establishes the core structure, workflows, and design system. But the real potential of the platform comes from how it evolves over time. The next phase is focused on making the system smarter, more automated, and more personalised.

What we'd build next
01
More AI agents, deeper automation

Expanding the Agent Factory with more pre-built agents and allowing teams to automate repetitive security tasks end-to-end — so security professionals can focus on decisions, not execution.

02
Smarter assess → deploy loop

Making the system more proactive — suggesting fixes earlier, auto-triggering actions, and reducing manual decision-making at every handoff point between modules.

03
AI that adapts to the user

Personalised guidance based on experience level — faster, more direct for experts; more contextual and supportive for users newer to security tooling. Same platform, adapted experience.

04
Trust & transparency in AI

Clearer visibility into what the AI is doing, why it's doing it, and giving users more granular control when needed — building on the transparency layer already in the MVP.

05
Scaling beyond four modules

Adding new capabilities — compliance, reporting, integrations — without breaking the core workflow logic. The design system and IA were built specifically so the platform can scale without fragmenting.
