Case Study
00 The Brief

A client, an open brief, and a lot of unknowns

A client came with a brief that was intentionally vague: build something meaningful in the education sector, at school level. No defined product. No fixed feature list. No validated problem to solve.

What they did bring was context — a rough understanding of the space, some early assumptions about what schools struggle with, and a genuine belief that there was something worth designing here.

The starting point

Not a product brief — a problem space. My job was to figure out what the product should actually be, and who it should serve, before designing a single screen.

02 Problem Framing

Narrowing the brief into an actual problem

At a surface level, the problem seemed clear: schools lack a unified system. But that was still too generic — it didn't explain where the breakdown happens, who is most affected, or what part needs the most attention.

Admins: operational chaos

Managing attendance, fees, schedules, and staff across multiple spreadsheets and apps with no single source of truth.

Teachers: fragmented workflows

Grades in one app, attendance in another, parent communication on WhatsApp. No unified view of student progress.

Parents: limited visibility

Relying on report cards twice a year or informal WhatsApp messages to understand how their child is doing.

Students: passive recipients

No ownership over their academic journey — homework, deadlines, and grades scattered across multiple channels.

Working hypothesis

If educational workflows are fragmented across multiple tools and manual processes, then bringing them into a unified, role-based system could improve efficiency, visibility, and coordination across every stakeholder in a school.

This hypothesis gave direction — but it still needed to be tested. Which meant one thing: research first, screens later.

03 Research

Understanding how schools actually work

Before designing anything, I needed to understand the space deeply — not just what the problems were, but why they existed, who was most affected, and what had already been tried. Research ran across five distinct methods over 2 weeks, each chosen to answer a different kind of question.

The sequence mattered: AI-augmented research built the contextual foundation, surveys gave directional data across all four roles, interviews revealed the emotional reality behind the numbers, field visits showed what actual usage looked like in real school environments, and competitive analysis mapped what already existed — and where it fell short.

03a AI-Augmented Research

Using AI to understand a system before entering it

Before any interviews or field visits, I used AI tools — Claude, Perplexity, and ChatGPT — to rapidly build a foundational understanding of the Indian school system. The goal wasn't to replace primary research. It was to go into the field already knowing the context, so I could ask sharper questions and spot things that don't show up in reports.

Why this matters

Walking into interviews about a system you barely understand wastes both your time and theirs. AI let me compress weeks of desk research into days — reading policy documents, EdTech reports, academic papers, and government data — so field visits could focus on what AI can't tell you: lived experience.

What I researched using AI
🏫

The Indian school system — how it actually works

India's education system isn't one system — it's hundreds. CBSE, ICSE, state boards, central schools, private unaided, government-aided. Each board has different exam patterns, different data requirements, different admin structures. I needed to understand all of them before designing for any of them.

🌾

Rural vs. urban school realities

AI research revealed a critical split: urban private schools use too many tools, rural government schools use none. The problem isn't the same across India — it varies dramatically by geography, funding, and digital literacy. This directly shaped who I prioritised in my primary research.

📜

History and policy context

NEP 2020, DIKSHA, PM eVIDYA, Samagra Shiksha — understanding the government's digital education roadmap told me what infrastructure was already being built at scale, and where the gaps in school-level management were being left unaddressed.

📊

Industry trends & market context

The Indian EdTech sector is projected to reach $10.4B by 2025 — but almost all of it is consumer learning (BYJU'S, Unacademy). School management infrastructure is chronically underserved. That gap is exactly where this project sits.

🌍

Comparable markets

Used Perplexity to research how Brazil and Indonesia — both large, fragmented education systems with similar structural challenges — had approached school digitisation. Found useful patterns around role-based access and parent engagement that informed the architecture.

💬

WhatsApp as infrastructure

Multiple sources confirmed what I later heard in every interview: WhatsApp is the de-facto communication layer between Indian schools and parents. It was never designed for this — but it works because nothing else does. Understanding this before field visits helped me ask the right questions about what a real replacement would need.

What the education system actually looks like

Behind every school system is a heavy operational load that rarely gets talked about — and a digital layer that was added on top without actually connecting any of it.

Every school — regardless of size, board, or location — is responsible for the same core operations. Managing who's enrolled and who shows up. Tracking how students are performing across subjects. Handling the money, the timetables, the staff. And somehow keeping parents, teachers, and students in the loop about all of it.

In most Indian schools, each of these areas runs completely independently. Attendance goes into a register. Grades go into a separate spreadsheet. Fees are tracked in a ledger or a billing app. Communication happens on WhatsApp. There is no thread connecting them — no moment where the admin, the teacher, the parent, and the student are all looking at the same information.

The result is a school that functions — just barely — through constant manual effort, repeated data entry, and informal workarounds. The operational load is real. And it falls most heavily on the people who can least afford the distraction: teachers in the middle of a lesson, admins buried in end-of-week reports, parents with no visibility until something goes wrong.

What exists vs. what's needed

What exists: Google Classroom, Microsoft Teams, WhatsApp — each solving one isolated problem, creating parallel workflows with no shared data.

What's needed: A connected system where data, communication, and tracking are unified — not fragmented across 5 different apps that don't talk to each other.

03d Field Studies

Going into the actual environment

Surveys and interviews tell you what people say. Field studies show you what they actually do. I visited a local school and spent time observing how admin work gets done in a real, live environment — not in a sanitised demo or a call.

What field studies actually are

You go into the user's real environment — office, classroom, admin room — and observe how they behave in context. You watch tasks happen in real life, take notes on the environment, tools, interruptions, and workarounds. You ask light questions without interrupting the flow. The goal is to see what people don't even think to mention in interviews — because to them, it's just normal.

What I observed
1

The admin desk runs on two systems simultaneously

A printed attendance register on the left, an Excel spreadsheet open on the right. Data is manually copied between them every morning. There was no single system — just two parallel records that had to match.

2

Teacher opened WhatsApp mid-class to message a parent

A student was absent. The teacher left the lesson mid-flow, opened WhatsApp, and typed a message to notify the parent. No app, no system — just a personal phone and a group chat that everyone uses for everything.

3

Fee collection was handled with a physical ledger

Cash payments were logged in a handwritten book. No digital record. No way for a parent to check if their payment had been received. The admin had to call parents individually to follow up on pending dues.

4

Students didn't know what homework they had

When asked, 3 out of 5 students in a classroom couldn't list all their pending assignments. They relied on friends in the WhatsApp group — the group that also had memes, voice notes, and unrelated chatter.

The key insight from fieldwork

The problem wasn't that people didn't want to use digital tools. It was that the tools available didn't fit the actual workflow — so people built workarounds around the workarounds. The informal system had become the real system.

04b Jobs To Be Done

What are users actually trying to get done?

Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) is a framework that shifts the focus from "what do users do" to "why do they do it." Instead of designing for features or tasks, you design for the underlying motivation — the job a user is trying to accomplish in a given moment.

The format is: When [situation] · I want to [motivation] · So that [outcome]. It captures functional need, emotional context, and expected result — all in one sentence. This keeps design decisions grounded in real human intent, not assumptions.

How I did it

After affinity mapping, I wrote JTBD statements for each role by pulling directly from research — interview quotes, observed behaviours, and survey responses. Each statement was then used to drive ideation: what would a good solution for this job actually look like?

✦ Example — Student
WhenI have multiple assignments and deadlines across subjects
I want toquickly see what I need to do next, in order of urgency
So thatI don't miss anything important or waste time figuring out where to start
→ So I ideated and designed for this job
📋 A consolidated task view — all subjects, all deadlines, one screen
🔔 Smart reminders that surface the next urgent task, not everything at once
🎯 A "What's due today" widget on the student home dashboard
⏰ Due date countdown so urgency is visible without mental calculation

04d Journey Mapping

Understanding the real experience

Journey mapping is a UX method that traces the full experience of a user completing a real task — from the moment they start to the moment they're done. It's not about screens or features. It's about what the user is thinking, feeling, and struggling with at every step — and where design can genuinely make things better.

📋 What users do

Step-by-step actions through a real task or workflow.

💭 What they think & feel

Emotions and motivations behind each action.

⚠️ Where they struggle

Friction points, drop-offs, and workarounds.

✦ Where design can help

Opportunity gaps where a better experience is possible.

How I did it

I chose the most frequent, high-friction tasks from each role — the ones that came up again and again in research. For each one, I traced the real-world workflow step by step, pulling in quotes, observed behaviours, and survey responses to fill in the emotions and pain points. I mapped journeys for attendance (Teacher), fee payment (Parent), grade visibility (Student), and operational overview (Admin).

05a Final Problem Statement

The real problem was never the tools

The original hypothesis — "schools need a unified platform" — was a solution dressed up as a problem. Research forced a reframe. The real issue wasn't the number of apps. It was that no one was responsible for keeping information in sync across roles.

What I assumed before research
"Schools need a single platform to replace the many disconnected tools they use."
This is a solution, not a problem. It assumes consolidation fixes everything — it doesn't explain why the disconnect happens or who's most hurt by it.
↓
What research actually revealed
"Every stakeholder in a school operates on a different version of reality — and no one is responsible for keeping those versions in sync."
The problem isn't the number of tools. It's that data doesn't flow between roles. The admin doesn't see what the teacher knows. The parent doesn't see what the admin sees. The student sees almost nothing.
Validated problem statement

Indian school stakeholders — admins, teachers, students, and parents — each operate in isolated information environments, held together by informal workarounds like WhatsApp and physical registers. The result is a system where the right information never reaches the right person at the right time, causing operational delays, academic blind spots, and a complete absence of student and parent agency.

05c Solution Directions

Where design effort should go

Not every problem is equally solvable through design. These four directions had the highest signal from research — the most frequent pain, the most participants affected, and the clearest path to a meaningful improvement. They became the north star for everything that followed.

01
Unified data visibility

The same data exists in every school — attendance, grades, fees — it just never flows to the people who need it. Design for role-aware access to a single source of truth.

02
Structured communication

Replace informal WhatsApp chains with a built-in communication layer — tracked, accountable, and contextual to school life. Fast enough to feel human, structured enough to be reliable.

03
Active student & parent roles

Both have been passive for too long. Giving them real dashboards — with live data, not report-card snapshots — fundamentally changes what it feels like to be part of a school.

04
Lightweight admin workflows

Admins are the human glue of a broken system. Automate the repetitive, aggregate the manual, and give them a clear operational overview — so they can run a school instead of chasing data.

💡Outcome: Four clear design directions — each grounded in research, each directly addressing a validated pain point. This is what ideation was built on top of.
07 Ideation & Exploration

Generate wide, then cut ruthlessly

Ideation isn't one thing — it's a sequence. I moved through four distinct phases: open brainstorming with zero filters, rapid sketching to force visual thinking, a feature flood across all roles, and finally clustering everything into a full module system.

09 Experience Structure

Building the backbone of the platform

Every decision up to this point — the research, the synthesis, the problem statement, the ideation, the MVP scope — was preparation. This phase is where it all becomes tangible. Structure is where you stop asking "what should we build?" and start answering "how will it actually work?"

Two things needed to be figured out before any screen could be designed. First: the user flows — the step-by-step paths each role takes through the system to complete real tasks. Second: the information architecture — how the product is organised, what lives where, and how four completely different roles navigate the same underlying platform without confusion.

🗺️ User Flows

Map the real paths users take — every step, handoff, and decision point — before touching a single screen.

🏗️ Information Architecture

Decide what lives where, how navigation is structured, and how four roles share one data system without overlap or confusion.

Why structure comes before screens

Jumping straight to UI without mapping flows and architecture is how you end up redesigning the same screen five times. Structure forces you to answer the hard questions first — what happens when, who sees what, and how does data move between roles. Get this right and the UI almost designs itself. Skip it and you're building on sand.

09a User Flow

How users actually move through the system

A user flow maps the exact steps a person takes to complete a task — every action, decision point, and handoff between roles. I designed flows for the three highest-friction, highest-frequency tasks in a school day. Not ideal scenarios — real ones.

What makes a good user flow

It's not about screens — it's about decisions. A good flow shows where control passes from one role to another, where things can break, and where a single action triggers multiple outcomes across the system.

Flow A — Attendance in detail

Attendance is the most frequent daily action across the entire school — and the most broken. Teachers mark it manually, admins follow up separately, parents find out only when something goes wrong. This flow fixes all three at once.

Teacher
Opens today's class attendance screen
One tap per student — present, absent, or late. The system pre-fills from the previous day so marking takes under 2 minutes.
↓
Teacher
Submits attendance
Single confirm action. Data is locked for the day and propagates immediately — no manual export, no shared spreadsheet.
↓
Admin
Sees real-time school-wide summary update
Admin dashboard refreshes automatically — class-wise breakdown, total absent count, flagged students. Zero manual aggregation.
↓
Parent
Receives absence notification
If child is marked absent, parent gets a push notification within seconds — with class, time, and the option to flag it as a known absence.
↓
Student
Sees their attendance record update
Student's attendance view reflects the day's status immediately — running count, monthly overview, leave status if applicable.

The key insight

Three roles, one teacher action, zero manual handoffs. The same data point — one student's attendance — does four different jobs simultaneously depending on who's looking at it.

09b Information Architecture

One system, four completely different views

Before touching a single screen, I mapped the entire IA — what content exists, how it's grouped, and how each role's navigation diverges from a shared data core. The goal wasn't four separate apps. It was one system with four smart, role-aware filters on the same underlying data.

💡
The core architectural insight

Most data in a school is the same data seen from different angles. The system doesn't need four databases — it needs four smart filters on one.

How I decided the navigation

The big question going into the IA was: should each role have a completely different navigation, or keep it consistent across all four? The answer was somewhere in between — and that "in between" took some careful thinking.

The navigation had to feel consistent enough that switching roles didn't feel like a different product, but different enough that each role only ever saw what was relevant to them. A teacher doesn't need fee management. A parent doesn't need a gradebook editor. Showing those would add noise — and in a school context, noise costs trust.

🔁 What stayed consistent

Bottom navigation with 4–5 tabs, a home dashboard as the entry point, a notifications icon in the header, and profile access in the same spot across all roles.

🎯 What changed per role

The tab labels, the home screen content, and the depth of each section. Admin gets reports and data management. Students get tasks and grades. Parents get visibility, not control.

⚖️ The principle behind it

Every role should feel like the app was built specifically for them — not like they're looking at a filtered version of someone else's dashboard.

🧠 What drove the hierarchy

Frequency of use. The most-used actions for each role went on the bottom nav. Everything else was accessible but not in the way.

10b Visual Language

The feel before the interface

Before touching a frame in Figma, I defined the visual tone of the product. For a school management system used by admins, teachers, students, and parents — the design couldn't feel like a consumer app, but it couldn't feel like enterprise software either. It needed to feel clear, trustworthy, and just human enough.

Typography

A clean sans-serif for body and data. Readable at small sizes, clear hierarchy between headings and labels — nothing decorative.

Colour

A primary violet for actions and highlights. Neutral backgrounds. Role-specific accent tones to subtly differentiate contexts without redesigning the whole UI.

Spacing & Layout

Generous padding. Card-based layouts for scannability. Bottom navigation for thumb-friendly mobile use across all roles.

Tone

Professional but approachable. Microcopy that explains rather than assumes. Empty states that guide, not just inform.

One colour per role — why it mattered

With four completely different user types on the same platform, colour became a subtle but important orientation tool. Each role gets a unique accent colour — used in their dashboard header, navigation highlights, and key action states. The rest of the UI stays neutral. This means a teacher and a student are clearly in different contexts without needing labels to tell them so.

The colours are distinct enough to feel intentional, but all desaturated enough to stay professional. None of them fight with the primary violet used for global actions — they complement it. The goal was differentiation without distraction.

10c Design System

One source of truth for the entire product

With four roles, multiple modules, and dozens of screens — building without a system would mean inconsistency everywhere. The design system was built in parallel with the UI, not after it.

1

Foundations

Colour tokens, typography scale, spacing grid, icon set, and elevation levels — defined once, applied everywhere.

2

Core Components

Buttons, inputs, cards, nav bars, badges, modals, and list items — each built with variants for all states (default, hover, active, disabled, error).

3

Role-Specific Patterns

Attendance marking UI for teachers, grade entry tables, fee status cards for parents, admin summary widgets — reusable patterns built for the specific contexts each role encounters.

4

Navigation System

Bottom nav with 4–5 role-specific tabs, a consistent header with notifications and profile access, and a "More" section for additional assigned modules.

12 Design Challenges

Where real life breaks your ideal flows

One of the key challenges was designing for real-world role complexity. In many Indian schools, roles aren't fixed. A teacher doesn't just teach — they might also handle admin tasks, manage a department like sports, or take on additional responsibilities depending on the school's size and structure.

The core tension

How does one user navigate multiple roles within the same system — without creating confusion, duplication, or a fragmented experience?

Instead of forcing rigid role separation, the solution was to introduce role-based permissions. The main admin can assign specific modules to teachers, which then appear dynamically in a dedicated "More" section of the navigation — allowing users to switch context without confusion, and without needing a separate login or account.

👩‍🏫
Example — The Multi-Role Teacher

A teacher who also manages the sports department needs access to both the teacher gradebook and admin-level event scheduling. Instead of two separate accounts, the admin assigns the Events module to this teacher — it appears in their "More" tab alongside their standard teacher navigation. Same login, same interface, extended access.

This was just one example. There were several such edge cases — where real-world behaviour didn't match the ideal flows I'd designed. These scenarios directly influenced:

🏗️ System Architecture

Permissions needed to be modular, not fixed.

🧭 Navigation Structure

The "More" tab — a flexible overflow for assigned modules.

🔧 Product Flexibility

Had to work for 200-student and 2000-student schools alike.

⚖️ Consistency vs. Context

Designing for a system means designing for exceptions.

14 Reflection

Beyond screens — this is still evolving

This project started as a simple brief — explore the education space. But it quickly turned into something much bigger. What I thought would be a UI problem revealed itself as a system problem — layered, fragmented, and deeply dependent on how different people interact with it every day.

01

Complexity doesn't need to be removed — it needs to be organised

The school system is genuinely complex. The solution isn't simplification — it's better structure.

02

Users don't struggle because they lack tools

They struggle because their tools don't connect. Integration is a design problem, not just a technical one.

03

Designing for multiple roles means constant balance

Every decision that helps one role can create friction for another. System thinking is essential.
